Rotating Diversity

Caltech has long been known not only for its rigorous academic environment but for its close-knit undergraduate community- a staple of the Caltech experience that we owe to the House system. Living in, and being a part of a House is a life-changing experience for many Techers that you won’t find in any other college, let alone in the outside world. Houses connect people of all grades, providing a social safety net and an opportunity for new students to learn from old students, all to help navigate the confusing and rigorous environment that is Caltech. With their traditions and historic spaces, the Houses manipulate space and time to connect students from the past, present, and future.

Three days after prefrosh first set foot on campus, they get to know the Houses during rotation. By the end of it, the prefrosh rank the Houses in order of preference and the Houses pick a list of particular prefrosh they especially want. Then, prefrosh are given their House assignments by what students lovingly refer to as “the algorithm”.

However, rotation could lead to a reduced amount of diversity within the Houses themselves, as it enables students to pick the Houses they get along with the most, and vice versa. Colleges advertise themselves as allowing their students to meet a diverse group of people. The demographic composition of Caltech as a whole has evolved, reflecting societal and institutional efforts to promote diversity and inclusion. However, do these efforts actually result in a more diverse student life experience?

What if Caltech ensured a diverse student life by making sure that each House has a diverse population? We already do this to an extent, as we try to make each House have the same gender ratio. Though, what about other demographic categories like athletes, LGBTQIA+ people, and under -represented minorities (URMs)? Should we ensure that these groups are evenly spread throughout the Houses? Would this foster a more diverse college experience, or erode House community and isolate people, by putting people in Houses they did not choose?

Creating our Toy Model

We begin with a population of 500 students, representing the demographic makeup of Caltech in this four-year instance. We gave three attributes to each student in the population: sports participation, Queer identity, and under-represented minority status.

Real-World Housing Choices

Universities and other formal organizations have been primarily concerned with structural diversity, otherwise known as the numerical representation of diverse groups admitted to their campuses. In diversity literature, researchers are focused on the impact of informal interactional diversity. Although these informal interactions with diverse peers can occur in many campus contexts, the majority of them occur outside of the classroom. Such interactions are facilitated by the House structure, including informal conversation, daily interactions in residence halls, House events, and casual social activities.

In the current housing system, specific Houses tend to have more of one population in them than others. For instance, most of the athletes tend to end up in one House. The Houses may also differ in terms of underrepresented minority, international status, or if they identify as queer or not.

For the sake of this toy model, we are assuming that certain groups will tend to pick one House over the others.

Determining Self-Selection in our Toy Model

Then for each individual, we generated a “House ranking,” which is what we assume the individual would have ranked the Houses.For instance, if someone’s ranking is C, A, D, B, then C is their most preferred house, while B is their least preferred.

The ranking is based on what groups they were a part of. So, if someone plays a sport, then they will rank House A first. If they are an URM, then they will rank B first. If they are queer then they will rank C first.

If someone belongs to multiple groups, then they rank the Houses associated with those groups first, the order of which is random. They will rank the rest of the Houses below those groups.

For example, if a student is an athlete and queer, then they will rank A and C before B and D. So, some possible rankings they could submit are: (A, C, B, D) or (C, A, D, B); etc.

Next, we rotated the population!

Our Rotation Algorithm

To rotate the individuals, we needed to create an algorithm that would maximize assigning people to their high priority house picks, while keeping the number of people in each house the same. This problem is similar to the stable marriage problem which uses the Gale-Shapley algorithm, but with an added constraint of keeping house sizes balanced. This algorithm is what matches medical students with residencies!

Self-Selection of Athletes

Caltech has had student-athletes since 1893 with 16 current intercollegiate teams and is a member of the NCAA Division III. However, within the last decade, the proportion of athletes among the undergraduate student body has increased to over 25%. This was due to an increasing role of athletics in admissions, which informs our toy model's proportion of athletes as well as our self-sorting algorithm. However, according to a 2024 article in the Tech, Caltech faculty stated that:

The preference that was given to athletes over the last few years will be gradually diminished, such that admissions will be based solely on academic merit and excellence.

This will have lasting impacts on Caltech's athletic population, among decreasing the number of student-athletes on campus. Using our toy model, we visualized how student-athletes are currently distributed among the Houses. We speculate this distribution influences their academic and social well-being, community cohesiveness, and belongingness.

Life-Style Factors

In our speculative model, having less student-athletes will increase their isolation due to being the minority in terms of sleep and work schedule and exercise lifestyle. For example, a different sleep schedule can cause potential tension between an athlete and non-athlete. It also affects the rest of the time they have outside of sports to spend in the House socializing with other non-athletic students. Less bonding time weakens their relationship to their Housemates, as well as their want to contribute towards the House community. A positive feedback loop forms as the more isolated they become, the harder it is for the athletes to become connected to their community.

Belongingness Across Scenarios

There are many different outcomes that would be effected by a change in the rotation algorithm. We picked one to model: "belongingness". “Belongingness” is simply a measure of how many people in a student’s house share an attribute of that student. For instance, if a student is an athlete, we looked at the proportion of people in the student’s House who are athletes, then threw that number into an equation, then added the result to the student’s belongingness measure.

In the equalized scenario, where an equal amount of each demographic group was placed in each House to maximize informal interactional diversity, the scores were lower across the board.

Though in both scenarios, athletes had the most belongingness, followed by URMs, and queers. This is because the more of a particular demographic there is, the more of them will be present in each House, even if they are equally spread among the Houses.

Reflecting on our Process

We were attempting to model an extremely complex system, which resulted in a lot of assumptions and leaps in logic. Even after narrowing down our parameters, we attempt to assign values to factors that are difficult to quantify, with many confounding variables. We had to cut down to only four demographic groups that exist, and that these groups have very simple, stereotyped, Housing preferences. In the process of building our model, we also experimented with adding more demographic groups and measuring different outcomes. Each variable we added (and attempted to add) helped us understand just how complicated of a system we are working with.

As such, our experiment should serve to make you think about how hard it is to consider all of the implications that simple changes to the rotation algorithm might entail. More importantly, it shows the difficulty of balancing the benefits of diversity, with the ethics of placing people in a House they might not want.

How Diversity Affects Society Past College

Pulled from diversity research, there are many positive effects from student diversity when done right. It ensures safe and inclusive public spaces past university, promoting gender-friendly cities and greater awareness of LGBTQIA+ spatial planning and queer spaces which is one of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

Armed with these insights, we consider how Caltech could leverage its data to create a more supportive environment for its students. Worthwhile future work would strengthen the importance of fostering an inclusive community through the Houses that supports students from all backgrounds.
